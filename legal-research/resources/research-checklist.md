# Legal Research Session Checklist

Run through this when you think you're done with your research session. It takes five minutes and catches the gaps that turn into problems later — the overlooked statute, the circuit split you didn't know about, the secondary source that would have saved you an hour.

---

## Search Coverage

- [ ] Ran both Terms and Connectors AND Natural Language searches for core issues
- [ ] Searched in the correct jurisdiction-specific database (not just "All Cases" the entire time)
- [ ] Checked both state and federal authority where the issue has a federal overlay
- [ ] Used the Key Number / Headnote digest system, not just keyword searches
- [ ] Searched secondary sources (treatises, law reviews, practice guides) — not just case law
- [ ] Searched statutes and regulations separately from case law
- [ ] Tried synonym and alternate phrasing searches for core legal concepts

## Authority Verification

- [ ] KeyCited (Westlaw) or Shepardized (Lexis) every case you plan to rely on
- [ ] No red flags on any key authority
- [ ] Yellow flags reviewed and understood — you know what the negative treatment is and whether it matters
- [ ] Checked for overruling, reversal, or statutory supersession on older cases
- [ ] Confirmed statutes are current — checked for recent amendments
- [ ] Checked whether any key authority is from a different jurisdiction and assessed persuasive value

## Issue Coverage

- [ ] The primary legal question is fully researched
- [ ] Sub-issues and prerequisite legal questions are identified and covered
- [ ] Procedural requirements are flagged (filing deadlines, notice requirements, exhaustion)
- [ ] Evidentiary standards are identified (burden of proof, admissibility rules)
- [ ] Affirmative defenses and counterarguments are accounted for
- [ ] Damages framework is researched where relevant (caps, categories, calculation methods)

## Depth Check

- [ ] Used "Citing References" / "Cited By" on at least two key cases to find related authority
- [ ] Checked for unpublished or recent opinions that don't show up in standard searches
- [ ] Looked for circuit splits or intra-state splits on contested issues
- [ ] Identified the most recent controlling authority — not relying solely on older landmark cases
- [ ] Reviewed at least one secondary source for a comprehensive overview of the issue area

## Organization

- [ ] Key cases are saved or flagged in a research folder on the platform
- [ ] Notes captured on why each key case matters — not just the citation
- [ ] Research trail is documented well enough that someone else could follow your path
- [ ] Gaps or unresolved questions are noted for follow-up
- [ ] Clear on which authorities are binding vs. persuasive in your jurisdiction

## Before You Move On

- [ ] You can explain the governing legal standard and its elements without looking at your notes
- [ ] You know the strongest authority supporting your position
- [ ] You know the strongest authority the other side will use
- [ ] You've identified the contested element — the one the other side will fight on
- [ ] You have enough to start drafting or report to the attorney with confidence

---

## How to Use This Checklist

1. Run through **Search Coverage** first — if you skipped an entire database type or search method, go back before you do anything else
2. Hit **Authority Verification** next — better to find the red flag now than in the reply brief
3. **Issue Coverage** and **Depth Check** catch the substantive gaps
4. **Organization** saves future-you from trying to remember what you found and why it mattered
5. **Before You Move On** is the gut check — if you can't hit all five, you're not done
